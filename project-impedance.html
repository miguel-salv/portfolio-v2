Project detail

Overview

The CMU Hacker Fab develops low-cost, open-source alternatives to expensive semiconductor manufacturing equipment. One of its core tools is a DIY RF sputtering chamber, where plasma conditions make the chamber impedance shift throughout a deposition run.

Traditionally, an operator has to keep tuning variable capacitors by hand to reduce reflected power. Commercial automated matching networks solve that problem, but their cost makes them a poor fit for a student-run fab. This project built a custom automated RF matching network to remove that manual tuning step at a much lower cost.

System Overview

The matcher replaces manual knob-turning with high-precision digital control. A Teensy 4.1 and custom T-network read forward and reverse power signals from the RF line, then drive high-torque stepper motors connected to air-variable capacitors.

The closed-loop controller uses gradient descent to search for better capacitor positions, stabilizing VSWR around 1.2 while maintaining 95W forward power for more than an hour without human intervention.

PCB Design

I designed a custom two-layer PCB around the Teensy 4.1, motor drivers, and active cooling. The board needed careful shielding and grounding so the microcontroller and drivers could remain reliable inside a noisy RF environment.

Sensor Integration

To measure VSWR, I reverse-engineered a commercial SW-112 VSWR meter and tapped into its internal toroidal sensing lines. I then built an analog sensing path into the Teensy's ADCs, using buffering so the measurement circuit would not pull down sensitive high-impedance lines or alter the RF behavior.

Firmware

The firmware processes digitized forward and reverse power, calculates improved capacitor positions, and drives the actuators in real time. I also built a local interface with an I2C OLED and rotary encoder so operators can switch between automated and manual tuning while monitoring VSWR and power telemetry.

Results

The final system removed a tedious manual tuning step from thin-film deposition work. The build cost was $636, giving the project an 80%+ cost reduction compared with entry-level commercial alternatives, and the open-source design makes it easier for other Hacker Fabs to reproduce.
